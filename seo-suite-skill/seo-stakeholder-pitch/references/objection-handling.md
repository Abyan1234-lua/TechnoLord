# Objection Handling Scripts

Full responses to the most common executive objections to SEO investment.

---

## "SEO takes too long — we need results now"

**What's really being said:** Our planning horizon is 90 days, not 12 months. I can't defend this investment in the next board meeting.

**Response:**
> "You're right that organic results compound over time — typically 6–12 months to meaningful scale. That's exactly why we should start now rather than later. Every month we wait extends the timeline to results and lets competitors deepen their advantage.
>
> Here's the key: paid search delivers instantly but stops the moment spend stops. SEO is the opposite — the work we do today generates traffic for years. We can run both in parallel: paid for immediate results, SEO for durable returns.
>
> For the 90-day horizon: here are the specific things we can show by then — [indexed pages, first rankings on long-tail queries, X% traffic lift on target pages]. It won't be the full payoff, but it'll be proof the channel is working."

---

## "We tried SEO before and it didn't work"

**What's really being said:** I spent money on this, didn't see results, and I'm not eager to repeat that.

**Response:**
> "That's a fair concern, and I'd want to understand what was tried before so we can do something genuinely different.
>
> Most SEO efforts that fail do so for one of three reasons: they chase the wrong keywords (high volume, high competition), they treat content as the whole strategy (it's not), or they don't have the engineering support to execute properly.
>
> What I'm proposing is different in a specific way: [name the key difference — product-led approach, programmatic content, technical foundation first, etc.]. The companies doing SEO well today aren't writing keyword-targeted blog posts. They're building something users want to find and optimizing for discovery second.
>
> I'd propose a bounded 90-day pilot so you can see the approach work before committing to full investment."

---

## "How do we know this will actually work?"

**What's really being said:** The ROI model feels speculative. I need more certainty before committing resources.

**Response:**
> "That's the right question to ask, and I want to be honest: SEO projections always involve some estimation. But here's what we can ground in data:
>
> [Data point 1]: Google Search Console shows we're getting X impressions for [query type] but only Y clicks — that gap is recoverable with specific page improvements.
>
> [Data point 2]: Competitor Z receives an estimated [X] monthly organic visits, which we can partially verify through [tool]. Their content and technical setup is [comparable / weaker than ours].
>
> My proposal: let's define a specific 90-day pilot with a clear success metric — [X organic sessions / Y conversions / Z pages indexed]. If we hit it, we expand. If we don't, we've learned something important for [bounded cost]. I'd rather run a real test than debate projections."

---

## "We don't have engineering bandwidth for this"

**What's really being said:** Engineering is already overcommitted and SEO isn't on the roadmap.

**Response:**
> "I understand, and I've tried to scope this to be realistic about engineering time. Here are three tiers of what we could do:
>
> **Tier 1 (0 engineering hours):** I can implement [X changes] in the CMS / through the marketing team directly. Estimated impact: [small but real number].
>
> **Tier 2 (2 engineering weeks):** Adding [specific feature/change] unlocks [estimated impact]. This is a self-contained ticket that doesn't require ongoing maintenance.
>
> **Tier 3 (full initiative):** The full [X]-week build delivers [maximum estimated impact].
>
> I'd suggest we start with Tier 1 to demonstrate proof of concept, then bring a Tier 2 ticket to the next sprint prioritization with real data behind it. That way we're not asking engineering to take a leap of faith — we're asking them to build on something that's already showing results."

---

## "Paid search works well for us — why would we change?"

**What's really being said:** Our current acquisition channel is performing. Why add risk?

**Response:**
> "Paid is working, and I'm not suggesting we stop. I'm suggesting we add a second channel that has different properties.
>
> The risk I'd flag: we're currently 100% dependent on paid acquisition. If CPCs rise (they typically do), if a competitor outbids us, or if a platform policy changes, our acquisition stops. That's a real concentration risk.
>
> SEO doesn't replace paid — it hedges it. The customers who find us organically convert at [comparable / better] rates, at a fraction of the ongoing cost. The highest-performing acquisition programs I've seen use both deliberately: paid for speed and targeting precision, organic for durability and scale.
>
> The ask isn't to reallocate paid budget — it's to invest in building a second channel while the first is still strong. Much easier than trying to build SEO when paid is no longer working."

---

## "This isn't the right time — maybe next quarter"

**What's really being said:** I'm not convinced enough to prioritize this right now.

**Response:**
> "I hear that, and I'd want to make sure the delay is intentional rather than something that just keeps getting pushed.
>
> Here's the cost of waiting: [specific competitor] has been building their organic channel for [X years]. Every quarter we wait, that gap grows and becomes more expensive to close. The best time to invest in SEO is before you need it — once you're in a traffic crisis, you're 12 months from recovery even with full investment.
>
> Can we agree on what would need to be true to move this forward next quarter? If there's a specific concern or missing piece, I'd rather address it directly than have this cycle through another planning round."

---

## "Can't we just hire an agency?"

**What's really being said:** I want this handled externally without consuming internal resources.

**Response:**
> "An agency can definitely help with specific tactical work — content production, audits, link building. But the highest-impact SEO work requires deep product integration, and agencies can't do that without internal ownership.
>
> The companies winning organically have someone internal who owns the channel and coordinates across product, engineering, and content. An agency can execute tasks; they can't own the roadmap, sit in product planning, or make judgment calls about what to build.
>
> I'd suggest: use an agency for [specific tactical work] while keeping strategy and product decisions internal. That's the model that tends to work. A full agency-owned SEO program without internal ownership is how you end up with content that ranks for nothing useful and technical changes that never get implemented."
