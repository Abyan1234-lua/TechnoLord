# Keyword Selection and Strategy Guide

---

## How to choose the right primary keyword

**Start with the user, not the tool.** Keyword tools show volume, not intent. Before opening any tool:

1. Write down what the target user would type when they have this problem
2. Think about where they are in the funnel — early (broad) or late (specific)?
3. Then validate that query in a keyword tool

**Criteria for a good primary keyword:**
- Matches the exact intent of the page
- Has enough search volume to be worth targeting (even 50–200/month is meaningful for the right audience)
- Is realistic to rank for given the site's current authority
- Is not already the primary target of another page on the site

**Avoid:**
- Choosing keywords by volume alone — high-volume head terms are usually too broad and too competitive
- Choosing keywords your product team uses instead of words your users use
- Ignoring zero-volume long-tail queries — keyword tools undercount these; Google Search Console reveals them after indexing

---

## Head terms vs. long-tail keywords

**Head terms** (1–2 words, e.g., "SEO", "project management")
- Very high volume, very high competition
- Broad intent — hard to serve well on a single page
- Take 12–24+ months to rank for without strong domain authority
- Best approached by building topical authority across many pages first

**Long-tail keywords** (3–6+ words, e.g., "how to do SEO for SaaS startups")
- Lower individual volume, but far less competition
- Much more specific intent — easier to satisfy with one page
- Faster to rank (weeks to months vs. years)
- In aggregate, long-tail drives more total traffic than head terms

**Brief implication:** For most pages, the primary keyword should be long-tail or mid-tail (2–4 words with specific intent). Reserve head terms for pillar pages with strong existing authority.

---

## The long-tail multiplier

A single programmatic or editorial page targeting a specific long-tail query may attract traffic from dozens or hundreds of related queries it never explicitly targeted. This is because search engines understand semantic relationships.

A page titled "How to reduce churn for SaaS companies" may rank for:
- "reduce SaaS churn"
- "SaaS customer retention strategies"
- "why SaaS customers cancel"
- "lower monthly churn rate"
- "churn reduction playbook"
...and many more.

**Brief implication:** The secondary keywords and semantic terms section of the brief isn't about stuffing. It's about mapping the full semantic territory so the writer covers the topic deeply enough to earn these related rankings naturally.

## Keyword cannibalization

Cannibalization happens when two or more pages on the same site target the same primary keyword. Search engines get confused about which page to rank, and both may perform worse than one good page would.

**Signs of cannibalization:**
- Two pages with the same or nearly identical title tags
- Two blog posts on the same topic from different dates
- A product page and a blog post both targeting the same transactional term

**How to avoid in a brief:**
- Before briefing a new page, check whether the site already has content targeting that keyword
- If it does: update and consolidate into one authoritative page rather than creating a new competing one
- Use a canonical tag if near-duplicate pages must exist for technical reasons

**Decision rule:** One primary keyword = one URL. If you need to cover the same topic from different angles, differentiate the primary keyword and the user persona clearly — don't split one page's traffic across two.

## Keyword placement — what matters

**High-weight placement:**
- Title tag (most important on-page signal)
- H1
- First 100–150 words of body content
- At least one H2

**Medium-weight placement:**
- Body copy (naturally distributed)
- Image alt text (where naturally applicable)
- URL slug
- Meta description (affects CTR, not rankings directly)

**Ignore / don't brief for:**
- Keyword density ("use the word X times per 1000 words") — this is an obsolete concept
- Meta keywords tag — has not been a ranking factor since ~2009
- Exact-match repetition — semantic variants rank just as well and read better

## Seasonal and trending keywords

Some keywords spike at certain times of year (e.g., "tax software" in Q1, "gift ideas" in Q4). Others respond to news cycles or industry events.

**For seasonal content:**
- Publish 6–8 weeks before the seasonal peak (Google needs time to index and rank)
- Use a stable URL (e.g., /best-tax-software, not /best-tax-software-2024) and update it annually
- Note any seasonality in the brief so the writer knows the timing context

**For trending topics:**
- Move quickly — ranking in the first 24–48 hours on a trending topic earns links and awareness
- Freshness is a ranking signal for news-like queries
- For evergreen pieces, focus on depth over speed
