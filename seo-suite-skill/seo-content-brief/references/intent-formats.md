# Intent Types and Content Formats

Use this to select the right content format based on what the user is actually trying to do.

---

## Informational intent

**User mindset:** "I want to understand something. I'm not ready to buy."

**What they type:**
- "how does X work"
- "what is Y"
- "why does Z happen"
- "X explained"
- "guide to X"

**Best formats:**
- Comprehensive guide (long-form, covers the topic fully)
- Explainer (shorter, one concept, clear and fast)
- FAQ page (multiple questions, scannable)
- Glossary / definition page (single term, tight answer, scales well programmatically)
- Comparison concept page (e.g., "X vs Y: the difference explained" — not product comparison)

**Brief priorities:**
- Answer the core question clearly in the first 150 words
- Depth matters — cover sub-questions the user will naturally have next
- Internal links to deeper cluster pages on related topics
- CTA: subscribe, explore related content, download a guide

**Length guidance:** 1,500–3,500 words for competitive topics; 400–800 for definition/glossary pages

---

## Commercial investigation intent

**User mindset:** "I'm comparing options and evaluating which is best for me."

**What they type:**
- "best X for Y"
- "X vs Z"
- "top X tools"
- "X alternatives"
- "X review"
- "is X worth it"

**Best formats:**
- Comparison page (structured head-to-head, clear verdict)
- Roundup / "best of" list (ranked options with criteria)
- Review (in-depth single product/service evaluation)
- Use case page (shows how X solves a specific problem for a specific persona)

**Brief priorities:**
- Be genuinely helpful — take a real position, don't hedge
- Lead with the verdict for scanners; back it up in the body
- Include comparison criteria the user actually cares about (not just feature lists)
- CTA: start trial, book demo, see pricing

**Length guidance:** 1,200–2,500 words; enough to be authoritative, not padded

---

## Transactional intent

**User mindset:** "I've decided. I want to do the thing."

**What they type:**
- "buy X"
- "X pricing"
- "sign up for X"
- "X discount code"
- "download X"
- "[brand] free trial"

**Best formats:**
- Landing page (conversion-focused, removes friction)
- Product/service page (features, proof, CTA)
- Pricing page (transparent, objection-handling, CTA)
- Case study (proof of value for someone ready to commit)

**Brief priorities:**
- Answer their specific intent immediately — they're not reading an essay
- Trust signals matter: reviews, testimonials, logos, guarantees
- Reduce friction on the CTA — make the next step obvious and low-risk
- Don't bury conversion elements under long informational copy

**Length guidance:** 400–1,000 words; concise, persuasive, conversion-focused

---

## Navigational intent

**User mindset:** "I already know where I want to go."

**What they type:**
- "[Brand name]"
- "[Brand] login"
- "[Brand] support"
- "[Brand] careers"

**SEO implication:**
Navigational queries are mostly won by being the brand. The content strategy here is brand-building, not topic targeting. Make sure your brand pages (homepage, login, support, pricing) are crawlable and clearly labeled.

Don't try to capture navigational queries for competitors — that's a paid strategy, not an organic one.

---

## Mixed intent

Some queries have multiple intent types in the same search. "Best project management software" is both informational (what's best?) and commercial (I might buy one). The content needs to serve both.

**Approach:**
- Lead with the most common intent for that query
- Include signals for the secondary intent further down the page
- Use your analytics to see how users actually behave on the page and optimize toward the dominant pattern

---

## Intent mismatch — the most common brief mistake

**Symptom:** A page written as a 2,500-word guide ranks but doesn't convert. Or a transactional page ranks low because it's too thin.

**Diagnosis:** The content format doesn't match what the searcher expects to find.

**Fix:**
1. Search the target keyword yourself
2. Look at what's ranking — what format do the top 5 results use?
3. Match that format (search engines have already validated what users want)
4. Then out-execute the competition on quality and depth
