# Seed Keyword Generation Tactics

A seed keyword list is only as good as its breadth. The goal is to capture how your users actually talk about their problems — not how your internal team describes your product.

---

## Tactic 1: Start with the problem, not the solution

Users rarely search for your product by name until they know it exists. They search for the problem your product solves. Map from problem → solution → product.

**Framework:**
1. List every problem your product addresses
2. Rewrite each problem as a search query a non-expert would type
3. These are your awareness-stage seeds

**Example (expense management software):**
- Problem: "I don't know where company money is going"
- Seed query: "how to track business expenses"
- Problem: "Finance team spends too long on expense reports"
- Seed query: "automate expense report approval"
- Problem: "Employees lose receipts"
- Seed query: "receipt tracking for small business"

---

## Tactic 2: Mine actual user language sources

The most reliable source of seed keywords is what your users actually type — not what your marketing team thinks they type.

### Online communities
Search these platforms for your core topic and harvest the exact language of questions and discussions:

- **Reddit**: `site:reddit.com "[topic]"` — look at r/ community questions. The thread titles are seed keywords.
- **Quora**: `site:quora.com "[topic]"` — question titles are exact keyword patterns.
- **Stack Overflow / Stack Exchange**: Technical topics — question titles and tag names are valuable seeds.

### Review and comparison sites
Read how customers describe the product and their problems in reviews:
- G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, GetApp (B2B software)
- Amazon, Yelp (consumer products/services)
- App Store / Google Play (mobile apps)

Extract phrases like:
- "I was struggling with..."
- "Before using this, I had to..."
- "The best feature is..."
- "I wish it could..."

These map directly to informational, consideration, and feature-specific keywords.

### Internal data sources
These are often the most accurate because they reflect your actual customers:
- Customer support tickets — look for recurring problem descriptions
- Sales call recordings and transcripts — the exact phrases prospects use
- Onboarding survey responses
- User interviews and usability research notes
- Live chat logs

## Tactic 3: Use SERP features as seed expanders

Type a broad seed into Google and observe what the search engine suggests — it's showing you actual search patterns.

**Sources to mine:**
1. **Autocomplete** — Type a keyword and observe the dropdown suggestions
2. **People Also Ask (PAA)** — The expandable question boxes in search results
3. **Related searches** — Bottom of the Google results page
4. **Google Trends related queries** — "Rising" queries reveal emerging demand

Each PAA question and autocomplete suggestion is a validated keyword. They represent real search volume.

---

## Tactic 4: Competitor content audit as seed source

Your competitors have already done keyword research. Their top-performing content reveals what's working in your space.

**Process:**
1. Identify 3–5 competitors who rank well organically in your niche
2. Use a tool (Ahrefs, Semrush) to pull their top 20 pages by organic traffic
3. Look at what those pages are about — the topics are your seed list
4. Look at the URLs and page titles — the language used is seed keyword data

**Important:** Don't just copy their keywords. Use their content as a map of validated demand, then ask what they've missed — those gaps are your opportunity.

---

## Tactic 5: Topic-to-keyword expansion (for blog/content sites)

If you're building editorial content at scale, use this structured expansion from broad topic to specific keywords:

```
Topic: [e.g. "email marketing"]
  ↓
Category: [e.g. "email marketing tools", "email marketing strategy", "email automation"]
  ↓
Sub-category: [e.g. "email automation for ecommerce", "drip campaign setup"]
  ↓
Specific queries: [e.g. "how to set up a drip campaign for abandoned cart emails"]
```

Each level represents a different level of specificity and a different stage in the user's research process. The most specific level is where quick-win keywords live.

## Industry-specific seed sources

**SaaS / Software:**
- Product Hunt comments and launch posts (problem statements in comments)
- Feature request boards (UserVoice, Canny)
- Help documentation search queries (what are users looking up in-product?)
- Competitor changelog pages (feature releases reveal user demand)

**E-commerce:**
- Amazon search suggestions for your product category
- "Customers also bought" and "frequently bought together" patterns
- Product reviews for your category on major retailers
- Pinterest search suggestions (strong for discovery-intent queries)

**Local services:**
- Google Maps Q&A sections for your category
- Yelp question threads
- Local subreddits and neighbourhood Facebook groups
- "Near me" modifier patterns: "[service] near me", "[service] in [city]"

**Healthcare and wellness:**
- WebMD and Healthline topic structures (validated informational architecture)
- Patient forum language (Cancer forums, chronic illness communities)
- Symptom-based queries — patients search symptoms, not diagnoses

**Finance:**
- NerdWallet, Investopedia topic structures
- "Personal finance 101" community language on Reddit (r/personalfinance)
- Life event triggers: "buying first home", "starting a business", "retirement"

**Education:**
- Course titles from Coursera, Udemy, Skillshare in your field
- University course descriptions
- "How to become a [role]" career pathway queries

---

## Seed quality check

Before moving to expansion, review your seed list:

- [ ] Does it include queries from all funnel stages (awareness, consideration, decision)?
- [ ] Does it include both broad terms and specific variants?
- [ ] Is the language user-native, not internal jargon?
- [ ] Does it include question-format queries (how/what/why/when)?
- [ ] Does it include comparison and alternative queries ("X vs Y", "alternative to X")?
- [ ] Does it include modifier variations (for [audience], free, template, example)?

Aim for 30–100 well-diversified seeds before expanding. More seeds = more comprehensive keyword universe downstream.
