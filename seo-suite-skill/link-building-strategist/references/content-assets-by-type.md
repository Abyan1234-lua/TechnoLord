# Content Assets by Type

*Sourced from: The Art of SEO — Enge, Spencer, Fishkin & Stricchiola (Ch. 7: Creating Link-Worthy Content and Link Marketing)*

---

## Content Format Selection Guide

Match content format to your target audience, distribution channel, and link goal.

| Format | Best distribution channel | Who links to it | Time to create | Link durability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Original research / survey** | Media outreach, industry publications | Journalists, bloggers, industry analysts | High | Very high (cited for years) |
| **Definitive long-form guide** | SEO, direct traffic, community sharing | Practitioners, educators, resource pages | High | Very high (evergreen) |
| **Interactive tool / calculator** | Niche communities, social shares | Publishers embedding utility tools | Very high | Very high |
| **Curated resource / top list** | Social sharing, community | Other resource curators, niche blogs | Low-Medium | Medium |
| **Data visualization / infographic** | Social sharing, media | Visual-first publishers, news sites | Medium | Medium |
| **Opinion / contrarian take** | Social, community discussion | Community members (pro and con) | Low | Low-Medium |
| **Case study** | Industry publications, direct outreach | Trade press, professional community | Medium | Medium |
| **Widget / embeddable asset** | Developer/webmaster communities | Sites wanting the utility for their audience | High | High if useful |
| **Video (hosted on site)** | YouTube, social | Media, bloggers | High | Medium |
| **Podcast / interview** | Podcast directories, social | Interview subjects, niche press | Medium | Low-Medium |

## Original Research — The Highest-Value Asset Type

Original research earns the most and the best links because it becomes a *primary source* that others cite. It's the only content type that cannot be replicated elsewhere.

### How to produce linkable research on a budget

You don't need a large research budget. Options:

| Method | Tools / approach | Time investment |
|---|---|---|
| **Survey your existing audience** | SurveyMonkey, Typeform, Google Forms | Low |
| **Analyze publicly available data** | Government data, industry reports, Google Trends, SEMrush | Medium |
| **Compile and analyze your own business data** | Internal analytics, CRM, transaction data (anonymized) | Low-Medium |
| **Run a study within your industry community** | LinkedIn polls, Twitter polls, email list | Low |
| **Commission a small research study** | Research firms, survey panels | High budget, low time |

### Research framing for maximum link attraction

- Lead with a **surprising, counter-intuitive, or definitive finding** — that's the hook journalists and bloggers use
- Express findings in **specific, quotable numbers** ("67% of X do Y" is more linkable than "many X do Y")
- Include a **methodology section** — it builds credibility and is required by serious publications
- **Release the full data** as a downloadable file — adds utility and attracts additional links from researchers
- Frame findings around a **trending debate or question** in your industry — hooks into existing conversations

## Long-Form Guide Structure

Definitive guides earn links because they become the canonical reference on a topic — the page practitioners and publishers send people to when asked a question.

### Elements of a link-worthy guide

1. **A specific, narrow topic** — "The Complete Guide to Backyard Beekeeping in Cold Climates" outperforms "The Complete Beekeeping Guide" because it owns a niche and is more useful to a specific audience
2. **Unique coverage angle** — What perspective or depth do you bring that existing guides don't?
3. **Comprehensive depth** — Every question a beginner or intermediate practitioner would have should be answered
4. **Current and accurate** — A guide with outdated information stops earning links and starts losing them
5. **Visual aids** — Diagrams, screenshots, tables; visual guides are more shareable and more linkable
6. **Easy to cite** — Use H2/H3 headers that make sections citable; each section should work as a standalone reference

## Link Bait — Content Designed for Emotional Response

Link bait triggers a reaction so strong that people want to share and reference it without any outreach at all.

### Emotional trigger types and approaches

| Trigger | Content approach | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Awe / inspiration** | Tell a genuinely remarkable story in your field | Behind-the-scenes of something extraordinary |
| **Humor** | Do something unexpectedly funny related to your niche | Blendtec's "Will It Blend?" — irreverent brand content |
| **Controversy** | Take a strong, defensible position against the prevailing view | "Why [conventional wisdom] is wrong" |
| **Usefulness** | Create something so practical it becomes a go-to reference | "The spreadsheet template I use for every X" |
| **Surprise** | Surface a fact or finding that shocks the audience | "19 things you didn't know about [topic]" |
| **Pride / community identity** | Create content that lets the audience signal membership | Annual "best of" award lists for the community |
| **Urgency / newsworthiness** | React to a breaking development before anyone else | First credible take on major industry news |

### Conservative brand considerations

Even conservative brands can execute link bait by:
- Anchoring it in a topic that is peripheral to the core commercial site (not the main product pages)
- Keeping it off the main navigation — the link bait section can be a separate resource area
- Choosing content that triggers awe, usefulness, or community pride rather than controversy or humor
- Citing their unique expertise as the authority behind the content

## Content Placement and Distribution

Where you put your content on the site affects how many links it earns.

### Placement principles

- **Accessible from main navigation or site hierarchy** — content buried 4+ clicks deep gets less organic discovery and fewer links
- **Standalone URL** — give link bait and resource content its own clean, memorable URL
- **No paywall** — gated content earns almost no external links; put your best assets in front of everyone
- **Shareable format** — long-form content should have a short, shareable URL and a clear title/meta for social previews

### Distribution checklist for new content

When publishing new linkable content, use this rollout:

```
□ Submitted to XML sitemap (search engines discover it)
□ Linked from internal pages (passes internal link equity)
□ Shared on owned social channels
□ Shared in relevant online communities (Reddit, LinkedIn groups, niche forums)
□ Directly outreached to 5–10 high-value prospects who would benefit from it
□ Sent to email list (if relevant)
□ Pitched to 2–3 industry publications for possible coverage
□ Submitted to relevant curated newsletters in the niche
□ Set a Google Alert for the title/topic to catch mentions
```

## Content Syndication Checklist

Before syndicating content to another site:

```
□ Is the syndicated version meaningfully different from the original?
   (Different angle, additional points, unique examples — not just a rewrite)
   
□ If the article is identical to one on your site:
   Does the syndicated version link to the original source article (not just your homepage)?
   
□ Does the anchor text of the return link relate to the article itself?
   (Not just a brand name link unrelated to the content)
   
□ Have you researched the host site's content needs and customized the pitch?

□ Is the host site genuinely editorial — does it have a real audience?
   (Not a content farm or link-for-link exchange site)

□ Will the syndicated version appear on a site that has higher or comparable authority to your own?
   (Syndicating to a low-authority site where you'd be seen as the original on Google is fine;
   syndicating to an authoritative site that ranks above you requires care with canonical signals)
```

## User-Generated Content as a Link Engine

UGC can produce link-attracting content at scale with relatively low direct effort. This includes:

- **Forums and community discussions** — active discussions attract links from community aggregators and related blogs; requires critical mass to sustain
- **User-contributed reviews and ratings** — research content, product reviews, expert reviews
- **Blog with third-party contributors** — invite respected community members to contribute; they will naturally link to their contributions from their own platforms
- **Photo / video galleries** — curate user-submitted visual content around your brand or niche

**Challenges:**
- Forums require significant traffic before critical mass of discussion forms
- All UGC requires moderation investment to prevent spam
- Quality varies — you are responsible for everything on your domain

**Contributor motivation hack:** Create a badge or recognition system ("Top Contributor," "Expert Reviewer") that contributors can display on their own sites, with a link back to your site. This converts contributors into willing link sources.
