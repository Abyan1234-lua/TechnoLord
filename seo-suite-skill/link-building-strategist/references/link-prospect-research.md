*Sourced from: The Art of SEO — Enge, Spencer, Fishkin & Stricchiola (Ch. 7: Creating Link-Worthy Content and Link Marketing)*

## Competitor Backlink Research Workflow

The most efficient link prospecting starts with competitors — they've already proven willingness to link in your niche.

### Step-by-step

1. **Identify your top 3–5 search competitors** for your primary target keywords (not necessarily business competitors — the sites ranking above you)
2. **Export backlink data** for each competitor using Ahrefs, Majestic, Moz, or SEMrush
3. **Find sites linking to multiple competitors** — these are your highest-priority prospects; they've already demonstrated editorial interest in your topic
4. **Identify content types** attracting the most competitor links — is it guides? Tools? Data? Interviews? This tells you what to create
5. **Look for "similar pages"** to top-ranked sites in your keyword markets. Sites consistently appearing in the link neighborhoods across multiple keywords are your topical community
6. **Build a prospect database** with columns for: domain, domain authority, topical relevance, contact info, outreach status

### Prospecting search queries

Use these search patterns to find link prospects beyond competitor backlinks:

```
# Resource pages
[topic] + "resources"
[topic] + "useful links"
[topic] + "recommended reading"
[topic] + intitle:"resources"

# Guest post opportunities
[topic] + "write for us"
[topic] + "guest post"
[topic] + "contribute"
[topic] + "submission guidelines"

# Broken link targets
[topic] + "resources" + "404"
[topic] + "links" + "this page no longer exists"
(use Check My Links or Ahrefs Site Explorer broken outlinks report)

# Directory categories
[topic] + directory
[topic] + "submit your site"

# Sponsorship / awards
[topic] + sponsors
[topic] + donors
[topic] + patrons
[topic] + "award recipients"

# Community hubs
[topic] + forum
[topic] + community
[topic] + association
[topic] + "industry group"
```

## Target Site Category Map

For any business, use this map to generate initial prospect category lists.

### Universal categories (work for most businesses)

| Category | Why they link | How to earn the link |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-competing businesses in your market** | Serve the same audience; cross-referencing is natural | Relationship outreach; partnership content |
| **Industry trade associations** | Maintain member resource pages and industry directories | Membership; contribute research or thought leadership |
| **Industry publications and trade press** | Cover news and trends in your space | Original data; expert commentary; newsworthy content |
| **Niche bloggers and independent publishers** | Build their reputation by surfacing useful resources | Great content + relationship building |
| **Government agencies (if regulated industry)** | Maintain resource pages for businesses in regulated areas | Submit content for consideration; develop educational resources |
| **University programs (if applicable)** | Maintain resource pages for students and researchers | Academic-style research; original data; scholarship programs |
| **Clients and partners** | Have an existing business relationship | Mutual linking in legitimate context; case studies |
| **Suppliers and distributors** | Already do business with you | Mutual linking; testimonials |

### Niche-specific categories

Identify 3–5 categories unique to your industry by asking: **Who are the information authorities in our field? Who do our ideal customers trust?**

Examples:
- **SaaS product:** Developer documentation sites, product review platforms, integration directory listings, software industry analysts
- **E-commerce (specialty):** Enthusiast communities, collector forums, hobbyist blogs, relevant niche media
- **Local business:** Local news sites, local business associations, city/region guides, local event sponsors
- **B2B services:** Industry analyst firms, professional associations, conference/event sites, LinkedIn groups linking to whitepapers

## Outreach Contact Research

### Finding the right contact

| Method | Best for |
|---|---|
| Site's Contact or About page | First point of entry for any site |
| LinkedIn (search [site name] + [editor / content manager / webmaster]) | Professional context; establishes credibility |
| Twitter profile of site's owner/editor | Personal/informal first contact |
| Email pattern tools (Hunter.io, Clearbit) | Scale prospecting when direct contact isn't obvious |
| WHOIS lookup | Domain owner contact when editorial contact is unavailable |

### Outreach CRM columns

Track every prospect in a spreadsheet or CRM:

```
Domain
Domain Authority (DA/DR)
Relevance (1–5)
Contact name
Contact email
First outreach date
Follow-up 1 date
Follow-up 2 date
Response (Yes / No / No response)
Link acquired (Y/N)
Anchor text used
Linking page URL
Notes
```

Rule: **Never send more than 2 follow-ups.** A third follow-up after no response crosses into spam territory and can damage your brand reputation in a tight niche community.

## Cold Email Templates by Tier

### Tier 4 (Low value) — Efficient volume

> Subject: Quick resource suggestion for [Page Title]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I noticed your page on [topic] at [their URL] — it's a great resource.
>
> I recently published [your content title and URL], which covers [brief description of what makes it valuable].
>
> If you think it's a good fit for your readers, I'd love it if you'd consider linking to it.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

---

### Tier 3 (Medium value) — Genuine personalization

> Subject: Your [article title] + a resource that might complement it
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I've been following your work on [topic] — [specific thing you genuinely appreciated about a recent piece].
>
> I just published [title + URL], which [one sentence on the unique angle or value]. Given what your audience cares about, I thought it might be worth a mention.
>
> Happy to hear your thoughts on it.
>
> [Your name]

---

### Tier 2 (High value) — Research-first approach

> Subject: [Specific angle relevant to their recent content]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I read your recent piece on [their article title] — specifically your point about [something specific]. It made me think about [connection to your work].
>
> I've been working on [your content title], which [what makes it genuinely different / what new perspective or data it brings]. I think it fills a gap in what's currently out there on [topic].
>
> Would you be open to taking a look? No pressure either way — I just think you'd find the angle interesting.
>
> [Your name, title, company]

---

### Tier 1 (Very high value) — Relationship-first, no ask in first contact

> Subject: Your work on [topic]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I've been reading your writing on [topic] for a while — particularly [specific piece that genuinely resonated]. [One sentence on why it stood out.]
>
> I work on [your brief intro]. No ask here — just wanted to say the work is great. Looking forward to your next piece on [topic you noticed they cover].
>
> [Your name]

*Follow-up 2–3 weeks later after genuine engagement (commenting, sharing their content):*

> Hi [Name],
>
> Following up briefly — I've been working on [your content title + URL]. Given your work on [their topic], I thought it might be interesting to you. Would love to hear your thoughts if you have a moment.
>
> [Your name]

## Business Relationship Link Opportunities

Don't overlook the most natural link opportunities — people you already have relationships with:

| Relationship | Link opportunity |
|---|---|
| Clients | Case study featuring their success → they link to it; testimonial on their site → link to yours |
| Suppliers and vendors | Supplier/partner pages; "powered by" links |
| Distributors and resellers | Include a link-back clause as standard in partner agreements |
| Industry association memberships | Member directory listing |
| Conference/event participation | Speaker bio pages; sponsor pages (nofollow risk) |
| Software integrations / API partnerships | Integration directory listings; partner pages |

These links require almost no outreach cost — they are unlocked through the normal course of business.

## Link Reclamation — Easy Wins

Before building new links, check for lost or unlinked mentions.

### Unlinked brand mentions

Search for your brand name in Google News, Twitter, and industry publications. Find mentions that reference your brand but don't link to you. Contact the author and ask them to add the link — the content is already written; you're just asking for a small editorial addition.

### Lost links (404 redirect targets)

Use Ahrefs → Lost Backlinks report. Find URLs on your site that no longer exist (404s) but had inbound links. Redirect those URLs to the closest relevant live page with a 301 to recapture the link equity.

### Broken links from credible sources

Set a Google Alert for your brand name. When a credible site links to a broken page on your site, fix it immediately.
