# Vertical SEO Specialist

Capture search visibility beyond the standard 10 blue links. Vertical search — local, image, news, video, product, blog/RSS — represents enormous traffic opportunity with far less competition than general web search.

The core principle: **Most sites optimize for web search and ignore everything else. Each vertical has its own indexing pipeline, ranking signals, and optimization levers. Mastering even one vertical can deliver more traffic than years of competitive web SEO.**

Why it matters now: since Google Universal Search (2007) and Blended Search across all major engines, vertical results appear *inside* the main web results. A top image, video, or news result can occupy prime SERP real estate even for keywords where you'd never organically rank.

## Phase 1: Local Search Optimization

Local search results dominate many geographic queries — often pushing all organic web results below the fold. If a business has any physical or service-area component, local search is non-negotiable.

### Why local search is different

Local search engines pull from a fragmented ecosystem of data aggregators, Yellow Pages directories, review sites, and the engines' own first-party listing systems. No single source controls everything. Consistent, correct data across all of these is the foundation.

The three main U.S. data aggregators that feed most local directories:
- **InfoUSA**
- **Acxiom**
- **Amacai**

Correct your listing at the aggregator level first. Accurate data at the source trickles down to hundreds of downstream directories automatically.

### Step 1 — Claim and verify direct listings

These are the highest-trust signals because you are validating data directly with the search engines:

| Platform | Action |
|---|---|
| Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) | Claim, verify, and fully complete your listing |
| Bing Places for Business | Claim and verify |
| Apple Maps Connect | Claim and verify |
| Yelp for Business | Claim and respond to reviews |
| Yahoo! Local | Claim and update |

When you validate an address directly with a search engine, that data is treated as more trustworthy than any aggregator source. If InfoUSA says "41 Temple Street" and Google's verified listing says "39 Temple Street," Google will use the verified listing.

### Step 2 — Audit and fix data consistency

NAP consistency (Name, Address, Phone) across all directories is a primary local ranking factor. Even small variations — "St." vs "Street", suite number formatting — create conflicting signals.

Run a citation audit using tools like BrightLocal, Whitespark, or Moz Local. Check:
- Is the business name identical everywhere?
- Is the address formatted consistently?
- Is the phone number the same (including area code formatting)?
- Is the website URL consistent and live?

For businesses with many locations, feed data directly to the top aggregators, Yellow Pages, and search engines via periodic bulk feeds rather than updating manually.

### Step 3 — Optimize the on-site local signals

The website itself must support local relevance. Key elements:

- **Title tags and H1 tags** — include business name, business type, and location: e.g., *"Acme Café: French Restaurant in Boston, MA"*
- **Contact/footer** — display the full NAP (street address, city, state, ZIP, phone) in crawlable HTML text on every page, not just in an image or JavaScript
- **hCard microformat** — encode address data using hCard markup so search engines can parse it as structured location data
- **Individual location pages** — for multi-location businesses, create a separate page per location (not one page listing all locations). Use each page's title, H1, and content to focus on that specific location
- **Location-specific differentiators** — include on each location page: map embed, hours, brand names carried, amenities, certifications, "in business since X", photos of the location

### Step 4 — Earn local-relevant links

Links are a ranking factor in local search, and locally-oriented anchor text amplifies the local signal:

- "Atlanta rental cars" is stronger anchor text for local rankings than just "rental cars"
- Best local link sources: local chamber of commerce, local newspapers, local charity events you sponsor, local college/school team sponsorships, local fraternal organizations, community event pages
- Sponsoring local charitable events (races, food drives, school teams) and requesting a linked mention on their website is one of the most effective and legitimate local link-building tactics

### Step 5 — Build and manage reviews

User ratings are one of the biggest ranking factors in local search — particularly in Google Maps. Google aggregates ratings from multiple third-party sources.

- Actively ask satisfied customers to leave reviews on Google, Yelp, CitySearch, Superpages, and Yellowpages.com
- Respond to all negative reviews promptly and professionally
- **Never** create fake reviews or pay for reviews — if detected, all reviews may be discarded and penalties applied
- **Never** attempt to manipulate competitor ratings

### Step 6 — Understand proximity to city centroid

Search engines historically ranked businesses closest to the city's geographic center first for general category searches. This is less dominant now but still a factor. If considering relocating, proximity to the city centroid is worth noting.

For zip code-specific searches: businesses closest to the zip code area centroid rank first.

### Additional local visibility tactics

- Add a blog feed map (feedmap.net) to any local blog — this signals local relevance and surfaces the blog to other locally-focused bloggers
- List in specialty Yellow Pages relevant to your demographic (industry-specific, language-specific, or interest-specific directories)
- Check local newspaper directory sites independently of the national guides — local papers often have strong domain authority in their city

## Phase 2: Image Search Optimization

Image search is underutilized by most SEOs. Competition is far lower than in web search, conversion from image visitors can be high in the right industries, and image results appear inline in Universal/Blended Search for many queries.

### Who should prioritize image search

Strong fit:
- E-commerce (product images are the product preview)
- Travel, hospitality, food and beverage
- Architecture, interior design, fashion
- Photography, art, creative services
- Medical and scientific content with diagrams

Weak fit: pure text/service businesses where images are decorative and visitors arriving from image search have low commercial intent.

### The challenge: search engines can't see inside images

Image ranking depends entirely on surrounding contextual signals — the image itself provides almost no direct text to rank on. Everything you do is about increasing the search engine's *confidence* in what the image contains.

The more consistently all surrounding signals reinforce the same topic, the higher the confidence — and the ranking.

### Core image optimization checklist

**Filename**
- Use the primary keyword in the image filename before uploading: `golden-gate-bridge-sunset.jpg` not `IMG_4728.jpg`
- Use hyphens as word separators (not underscores)
- Keep it descriptive of what the image actually shows

**Alt attribute**
- Every image must have a relevant, descriptive alt attribute
- Correct syntax with quotes (required when alt text contains spaces): `<img alt="Golden Gate Bridge at sunset" src="...">`
- Missing quotes cause search engines to drop all words after the first — a widespread, easily-fixed error
- Write as a description for someone who cannot see the image, not as a keyword list

**URL / IMG SRC**
- Avoid query strings in image URLs where possible (`/images/golden-gate-bridge.jpg` beats `/img.php?id=4728`)
- If query strings are unavoidable, limit to 2–3 parameters
- Ensure the image directory is not blocked in `robots.txt`

**Page-level context**
- The page title, H1 heading, and body content must all reinforce the image topic — this multiplies confidence in the image's subject
- Place descriptive captions directly below images — this text is strongly associated with the image by both users and crawlers
- The text immediately before and after the image carries extra weight
- Don't store images in sidebars, headers, or footer navigation elements — engines treat those positions as irrelevant site decor

**Image quality and format**
- Images must look good at thumbnail size — high contrast and clear subject reads better in small format
- Turn off embedded thumbnail saving in Photoshop and other editors — it bloats file size and can cause compression issues when search engines generate their own thumbnails
- Square images make best use of Google Product Search's 90×90px thumbnail space
- Ensure the server configuration allows images to be hot-linked from other domains (needed for search engine result page display)

**Duplicate image handling**
- If using licensed/manufacturer images that appear on other sites, modify the file size, resolution, or aspect ratio before uploading — even trimming a few pixels changes the file fingerprint and avoids duplicate image filters
- Add a watermark with your site URL on images likely to be redistributed — it maintains branding value as images spread

**Copyright**
- Only use images you have legal rights to display
- Exercise caution with Wikimedia Commons and public domain sites — provenance is not always reliable

### Flickr and image sharing sites

Flickr has strong domain authority and can rank independently in web search for image-related queries — potentially giving you a second or third result page listing for the same keyword.

Flickr optimization:
- Add tags to every photo (as many accurate keywords as possible); wrap multi-word tags in quotes: `"pickup truck"`
- Write a descriptive title for each photo
- Write a description in the Description field — include a contextual link back to the related page on your site
- Create thematic sets and add each photo to relevant sets
- Browse Groups for relevant thematic collections and join them; add your photos to applicable groups
- If location-specific, geotag the photo
- Make photos publicly viewable
- License photos under Creative Commons where appropriate
- The more optimized photos you post, the more cumulative authority your Flickr profile builds

## Phase 3: News Search Optimization

Getting into Google News and Yahoo! News puts your content in front of the largest news audiences on the web, drives significant traffic spikes per story, and creates indirect link-building opportunities as journalists and bloggers discover your content through news search.

**Prerequisite:** News search optimization requires a genuine, sustained commitment to publishing news or feature content. The minimum viable threshold is ~10 articles per week. If you cannot sustain this volume, news search is not the right vertical to pursue.

### Google News vs. Yahoo! News: the key difference

| | Google News | Yahoo! News |
|---|---|---|
| Volume bar | Lower — accepts a wider range of decent news sources | Higher — requires significant unique, high-value content |
| Format requirements | More specific technical requirements | More lenient; RSS feed preferred |
| Traffic opportunity | Very large | Very large (historically largest news portal) |

Best practice: meet Google's stricter technical requirements first — Yahoo!'s looser requirements will be satisfied automatically.

### Content requirements

News search engines are looking for:
- **News stories or feature stories** — not marketing content, product pages, or blog posts about your company
- **Consistent volume** — a minimum of 10 new articles per week; engines evaluate sources, not individual articles
- **Traditional news structure** — keyword-rich headline, strong opening paragraph leading with the main point, descending importance through the body, strong concluding summary
- **Named authors** — Google News requires editorial credibility signals including named editors/authors (minimum ~3 identified authors)

### Technical requirements for Google News

- All articles must be at **static, permanent URLs** — the URL and content must not change after publication
- URLs must be accessible via **standard HTML text links** (not JavaScript rendering or redirect chains)
- Content must be in **standard HTML** — no PDFs, multimedia, or frames
- Use **UTF-8 character encoding**
- Create and submit a **Google News Sitemap** via Google Search Console — this is different from a standard XML sitemap; it uses a specialized format (or RSS/Atom feed) and speeds up article discovery significantly
- Include a **history of the site and traffic statistics** in your submission form
- Include a **2–3 sentence bio for each of your editors/authors** on the news page or linked from it

### Technical requirements for Yahoo! News

- Submit an **RSS feed** — Yahoo! News ingests and indexes content via RSS
- Ensure the feed includes full article text where possible (not just summaries)
- The feed must represent high-value, unique content not already available from other sources

### After acceptance: maximizing news search performance

- Use **keyword-rich headlines** — the headline is the primary ranking signal; write for the search query as well as for human readers
- Use **News Sitemaps or RSS pings** to notify the news engine of new stories immediately upon publication — Pingomatic.com pings major feed services automatically
- Implement the Google News Sitemap as an RSS or Atom feed with a ping mechanism for fastest discovery
- Include a **strong opening paragraph** — in news format, the most important information leads; don't bury the news angle
- Each story's URL should remain live permanently — news engines track source history and stability

> See [references/news-and-blog-seo.md](references/news-and-blog-seo.md) for submission URLs, sitemap formats, and editorial standards.

## Phase 4: Blog Optimization

Blogs combine the on-page SEO fundamentals of web search with a content velocity and social distribution model that no static site can match. A well-optimized blog is both a search traffic engine and a link acquisition machine.

### Structural blog optimizations

These are the most impactful technical changes — fix them before creating new content:

**Title tags**
- Override default CMS-generated title tags with custom, keyword-rich ones per post
- Default blog titles often prepend the site name or use the category name — override with a descriptive post-level title
- Use a plugin like SEO Title Tag (for WordPress) or your SEO plugin's custom title field

**URLs (post slugs)**
- Rewrite URLs to contain target keywords; use hyphens as word separators (not underscores)
- Remove dates from URLs — date-based URLs look stale over time and hurt evergreen content
- If you worry about URL length, use a slug shortener plugin to trim to the most important keywords
- If migrating from one blog platform to another, 301-redirect every old URL to its new equivalent — each old URL has built-up link equity

**Canonical redirect**
- Implement a 301 redirect from `http://yourblog.com` to `http://www.yourblog.com` (or vice versa) — this must work for all internal pages, not just the homepage

**Category pages**
- Assign each post to relevant categories — category names appear in title tags; make them keyword-rich
- Use a "sticky post" per category page: a pinned intro/overview post that adds keyword-rich introductory copy to the top of category pages, compensating for the thin content problem most category pages have

**Internal linking**
- Internally link from new posts back to older, relevant posts using keyword-rich anchor text — not "here" or "this post"
- Use a Related Posts plugin (e.g., Yet Another Related Posts Plugin for WordPress) to surface related content and distribute internal link equity automatically
- Link to your top 10 best posts regularly, ensuring each links forward and backward to adjacent posts with descriptive anchor text

**Author profile pages**
- For multi-author blogs, create individual author profile pages — authors will link to their profile from their own sites, earning you inbound links
- Offer per-author RSS feeds — this deepens reader loyalty and encourages direct subscription

### Content and anchor text optimization

- Make the post title a clickable link to the permalink page — never make "Permalink" the only navigational link to a post
- Use emphasis tags (`<strong>`, `<em>`) deliberately within post bodies — these still provide mild relevance signals
- Apply `rel="nofollow"` on all comment and trackback links — these are unreliable editorial endorsements and should not pass link juice
- Audit your inbound links using a tool like Ahrefs or Moz — when you identify inbound links using weak or irrelevant anchor text, reach out to request an anchor text revision

### Tags and tag clouds

Tag clouds create a link navigation system that is useful for both users and crawlers — they surface popular topics and create keyword-rich internal anchor text at scale. Use them judiciously; over-tagging creates low-quality duplicate content.

## Phase 5: RSS Feed Optimization

RSS is far more than a blog syndication tool. It applies to any regularly updated content: news alerts, new product arrivals, upcoming events, top sellers, clearance items, new tools, search results, project activity — any content that benefits from subscription and feed-based discovery.

### Core RSS optimization principles

**Use full article text in feeds, not summaries**
Readers prefer full-text feeds — they can read without clicking through. This builds trust and subscription loyalty. The long-term traffic relationship with subscribers outweighs the short-term click loss.

**Multiple feeds by content type**
Offer feeds segmented by category, content type, author, or tag. This allows subscribers to follow only what they want and signals topical organization to feed aggregators.

**Podcast/media enclosures**
Feeds containing media enclosures (audio, video) qualify for podcast-specific directories and media-focused RSS search engines — additional vertical channels most feeds never pursue.

**Subscription friction**
- Implement one-click subscription via "Add to My Yahoo!", Bloglines, Google Reader, and other aggregator buttons
- Add `<link rel="alternate" type="application/rss+xml">` autodiscovery tags in the `<head>` of every page so browsers and feed readers automatically detect your feed

**Own your feed URL**
Do not build your feed infrastructure on a third-party provider's URL (e.g., FeedBurner). Readers and subscribers who follow that URL are tied to the provider, not to you. Host your feed at your own domain URL. (The comparison in the book: putting Earthlink.net in your email address on a business card.)

### RSS tracking and measurement

**Tracking reads (open rates)**
Embed a 1-pixel tracking GIF (web bug) inside the `<content:encoded>` container to measure how many subscribers open/read each item — the same technique email marketers use for open rate tracking.

**Tracking click-throughs**
Replace all URLs in `<link>` containers with click-tracked redirect URLs. This reveals which feed items drive the most traffic.

**Critical:** If using FeedBurner or SimpleFeed for click tracking, configure them to use **301 redirects** — both default to 302, which does not pass link juice from syndicated sites back to your destination URL.

**Tracking subscriber count**
Use a service like FeedBurner to categorize visiting user agents: bots and browsers don't count as subscribers; aggregator requests (which may represent many subscribers per request, as revealed in the user agent string, e.g., `Bloglines/2.0 (…; xx subscribers)`) do.

### Protecting your feed

- Scan server logs for referrers that are stripping your links or applying `rel="nofollow"` to them — these sites are consuming your content without passing link credit. Block their IP addresses from feed access.
- If you allow feed syndication on other websites, use standardized affiliate feeds with tracking codes and 301-redirect those to canonical URLs to maintain link juice flow.

### Pinging feed services

Use **Pingomatic.com** to ping all major feed services simultaneously on every update. For Yahoo! specifically, ping directly: `http://api.my.yahoo.com/rss/ping?u=http://www.yourdomain.com/rss`

> See [references/news-and-blog-seo.md](references/news-and-blog-seo.md) for a full RSS optimization and measurement setup guide.

## Phase 6: Video Search Optimization

YouTube is the second largest search engine in the world. Video results from YouTube and other hosting platforms appear inline in Google web search through Universal Search. Optimizing video content captures SERP real estate that text-only competitors cannot touch.

**The fundamental challenge:** Video search engines cannot see inside the video itself (with limited exceptions: face detection, text on screen, and experimental voice recognition). Everything depends on external signals — metadata, surrounding text, inbound links, and platform engagement signals.

### Three video optimization scenarios

Run all three in parallel for maximum coverage.

**Scenario 1: Videos hosted on your own site**
Search engines like Truveo crawl your site and discover hosted video files. Optimization levers:
- Write keyword-rich, compelling descriptions on the page where each video lives — this is standard HTML, so use full on-page SEO
- Store video files in a dedicated directory to make crawler discovery efficient
- For Flash video players, include a direct link to the raw video file (AVI, WMV, MOV) alongside the embed — different video search engines prefer different formats
- Submit a **Video Sitemap** to Google Search Console — this is the most reliable way to ensure video content is discovered and indexed

**Scenario 2: RSS / MRSS feed submission**
Submit an RSS or MRSS feed to third-party video search engines (Yahoo! Video, Blinkx). Per submission, provide:
- Title
- Description (keyword-rich)
- Keywords / tags
- Thumbnail
- Category
- **Full transcript** — this is the highest-value addition; it turns an opaque video file into a fully indexable keyword document

**Scenario 3: Upload to video platforms (YouTube, Vimeo, etc.)**
This is where the majority of video search traffic lives. Key platforms:
- YouTube
- Vimeo
- Dailymotion
- Metacafe
- Flickr (max 90 seconds)
- Yahoo! Video

Use **TubeMogul** to distribute a single upload to multiple platforms simultaneously, plus get unified analytics.

### YouTube optimization in detail

YouTube has its own search ranking system independent of Google web search. Optimize each video upload:

**Title**
- Primary ranking factor for click-through rate
- Include the target keyword near the start
- Keep under 62 characters (YouTube's display limit)
- Make it catchy — a compelling title drives views; views drive ranking
- Include the word "video" naturally where it fits — many searches include this word

**Description**
- Include the target URL in the first line (YouTube makes full URLs clickable — include `http://`)
- Write a full keyword-rich description of the video content
- Link to related content on your site — drive qualified traffic back
- Include secondary keywords naturally in the body of the description

**Tags**
- Use all available tag space; enter as many accurate keywords as possible
- Tags power YouTube's suggested video and category search results
- Spread tags across multiple clips (don't duplicate the exact same tag set on every video)
- Use adjectives that describe mood or style (helps discovery by users browsing by feel)
- Match your most important tags to your title and description

**Thumbnail**
- Thumbnails have a major impact on click-through rate — a compelling thumbnail gets more views
- The process for setting a custom thumbnail differs by platform; invest the effort on each
- Ensure the subject is clearly identifiable at small sizes; avoid dark, low-contrast images

**Engagement features**
- Enable ratings — search engines weight user ratings in video rankings; high ratings also drive viral spread
- Enable comments and video replies — social engagement signals platform quality
- Watermark commercial videos with your site URL — branding persists as videos get shared and embedded
- End videos with a "send to a friend" prompt or call-to-action

**File and format**
- Keep video files under 100 MB for compatibility across platforms (YouTube accommodates up to 1 GB / 10 minutes as of publication)
- Include the keyword in the video filename
- Pick one primary format for SEO visibility; offer multiple formats to users for their preference
- Keep videos under 5 minutes unless the subject genuinely demands more — shorter videos have higher completion rates and better engagement signals

**Distribution**
- Submit to all relevant video platforms (use TubeMogul for efficiency)
- Submit videos to appropriate MRSS feeds
- Enable embedding — embeds spread your video and create indirect link opportunities
- Use social bookmarking tools to increase initial velocity of views and social signals
- Include your video in your RSS feed using media enclosures

### The YouTube link-building caveat

Most links generated by a YouTube video point to YouTube, not to your site. The direct link equity benefit to your domain is limited. Counter this by:
- Creating on-site landing pages that feature the video and add complementary content — links to those pages flow to your domain
- Including your URL in the video description (clickable) to drive qualified traffic
- Building buzz that motivates third-party bloggers and media to link to your site when writing about the video

## Phase 7: Product Search Optimization

Google Product Search (now Google Shopping) can surface your product listings inside general web search results as a onebox — appearing at the top, middle, or bottom of the page. For product-based businesses, this is high-intent real estate.

### Getting into Google Product Search

1. Create a **product data feed** formatted to Google's specifications
2. Submit the feed via **Google Merchant Center** (formerly Google Base)
3. Update the feed as frequently as possible — major retailers update daily

Only true physical/tangible products qualify for Google Product Search. Digital products, services, flights, hotels, and real estate are accepted by Google Base but will not appear in Product Search.

### Feed optimization

**Populate every available field** — title, brand, category, color, size, price, condition, UPC. More complete feeds match more search queries.

**Titles**
- Write descriptive, accurate titles that reflect how users search
- For competitive products, use long-tail terms in titles — pick phrases with some search volume and conversion potential
- For large catalogs where hand-researching every product is not practical, extract unique product attributes (model number, color, material, size) and include them in the auto-generated title
- Product name + brand name in the title is typically the most effective combination

**Images**
- Always include product images — Google Product Search strongly prefers listings with images; Bing will not show products without images
- Format for Google's 90×90px thumbnail: shoot products square, fill the frame, use high contrast against a clean background
- No muddy, low-contrast images — they fail to read at thumbnail size

**Seller and product ratings**
- Seller ratings from DealTime, NexTag, PriceGrabber, ResellerRatings, and Shopzilla directly influence rankings
- Product ratings from sources like Epinions.com contribute to product-level ranking signals
- Manage your ratings actively across all contributing platforms

**Other ranking factors**
- Domain authority of your main website
- Your site's general web search ranking for the keyword
- Whether any products are flagged as "adult" — if so, all products may be filtered from SafeSearch results
- Number of users who add your products to their Google Shopping lists

**Performance tracking**
Use Google Merchant Center's Performance tab to track clicks per product, active vs. inactive items, and feed health. Download CSV reports to identify underperforming listings.

## Phase 8: Mobile Search Optimization

Mobile search runs on a separate crawler (`Googlebot-Mobile`) and separate ranking algorithms from desktop web search. As mobile device usage has grown to dominate internet traffic, mobile search optimization has become a prerequisite, not an add-on.

### Mobile-specific ranking factors

| Factor | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Page size** | Target <20 KB per mobile page — mobile connections are slower; heavy pages are penalized |
| **Document type** | Use XHTML Mobile 1.0 DOCTYPE for dedicated mobile pages |
| **Character encoding** | UTF-8 |
| **Image formats** | JPEG and GIF for maximum compatibility |
| **Title tags** | Short; include the word "mobile" in title or H1 where relevant |
| **Body copy** | Keep concise — smaller screen = less visible text |
| **Mobile Sitemaps** | Submit a Mobile Sitemap via Google Search Console |

### Content negotiation vs. separate mobile URL

Two implementation approaches:

**Content negotiation (recommended when possible):** When `Googlebot-Mobile` crawls your site, it signals via the HTTP Accept request header that it prefers mobile content. If your server is configured for content negotiation and you have a mobile version, both mobile users and mobile crawlers receive the mobile version at the same URL — no redirect needed.

**Separate mobile URL:** Use a distinct URL (e.g., `m.yourdomain.com`) for marketing and promotional use cases, or when you need to drastically reduce HTML payload. Well-known examples: `m.flickr.com`, `m.facebook.com`, `mobile.yelp.com`.

Apply all standard SEO best practices — mobile algorithms evaluate the same quality signals, just optimized for smaller form factors and slower connections.

## Common Traps to Call Out

Proactively flag these when they appear:

- **Treating vertical search as optional** — for many queries (local, product, news), vertical results dominate the first page. Ignoring them cedes massive traffic to competitors who optimize.
- **Setting up a Google Business Profile and never touching it again** — local listings decay as data aggregators push conflicting information. Review and refresh listings quarterly.
- **Missing alt attributes or using alt attributes without quotes** — one of the most widespread technical errors; causes search engines to drop alt text after the first word.
- **Storing images in navigation or sidebar** — engines treat these as site furniture, not content, and ignore them for image search.
- **Using FeedBurner or SimpleFeed default redirects for RSS** — both use 302 by default; configure to 301 or link juice from syndication is lost.
- **Hosting all video on YouTube with no on-site presence** — YouTube gets the links; your domain gets nothing. Always complement YouTube with an on-site landing page.
- **Submitting to Google News without meeting the 10 articles/week threshold** — news engines evaluate sources, not individual articles. Below-threshold submissions are rejected.
- **Using identical NAP formatting inconsistently** — a period after "St" in one directory and not another registers as a discrepancy. Full consistency is the standard.
- **Ignoring review management** — user ratings are among the strongest local ranking signals. Not asking satisfied customers to review you is leaving ranking power on the table.
